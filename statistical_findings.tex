\section{Results}
This report provides a master's level critical analysis of the statistical findings regarding developer behavior within a codebase. The research investigates how developer roles (\textit{Founder} vs. \textit{Late Joiner}) and tenure (\textit{Transient, Moderate, Sustained}) influence the breadth of their technical footprint, measured through packages, classes, and methods touched.

\subsection{Analysis of Welch's t-test Results}
The study utilizes Welch's t-test and the Mann-Whitney U test to account for unequal variances and sample sizes between primary roles.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Founder vs. Late Joiner:} There is a statistically significant difference ($p < 0.005$) across all components (packages, classes, and methods). Founders consistently demonstrate a higher mean ($\mu$) of components touched compared to Late Joiners. 
    \item \textbf{Moderate vs. Sustained:} While packages showed significant differences ($p < 0.0001$), the differences in classes and methods were not statistically significant ($p > 0.05$ for Welch's test). This suggests that while "Sustained" contributors stay longer, their breadth of impact on specific code structures in early stages may mimic that of "Moderate" contributors.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Two-Way ANOVA: Interaction of Role and Tenure}
The ANOVA models evaluate the effects of \textit{Role}, \textit{Tenure}, and their interaction $(\alpha\beta)_{ij}$.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Breadth of Impact:} For the first five commits, the interaction between Role and Tenure significantly impacts classes ($p = 0.0345$) and methods ($p = 0.0079$), but not packages. 
    \item \textbf{Temporal Consistency:} As the commit history increases to 20 commits, the significance of Tenure as a standalone factor increases (e.g., $p = 0.0000$ for classes and methods), indicating that long-term retention is a stronger predictor of code breadth than initial role alone.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Predictive Strategy: Logistic Regression}
The logistic regression model identifies whether early-stage behaviors can predict if a developer will become a \textit{Sustained Contributor}.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Odds Ratios (OR):} All analyzed components yielded an $OR > 1$. For example, the Packages Count at 5 commits has an OR of approximately $1.0578$.
    \item \textbf{Interpretation:} Since $OR > 1$, an increase in the number of components touched during the initial stages of joining a codebase significantly increases the likelihood of long-term developer retention. The high predictive accuracy is further validated by AUC-ROC metrics.
\end{itemize}

\section{Summary of Findings}
The synthesis of these results suggests a "breadth-first" indicator for developer retention. 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Foundational Dominance:} Founders maintain a significantly wider technical footprint than those who join later.
    \item \textbf{Early Indicators:} The variety of methods and classes touched within the first 5 to 10 commits is a statistically significant predictor of whether a developer will transition into a sustained contributor.
    \item \textbf{Managerial Implication:} To encourage long-term retention, onboarding processes should perhaps focus on exposing developers to a wider variety of system components early in their tenure.
\end{enumerate}
