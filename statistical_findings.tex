\section{Summary of Findings}

\subsection{Comparative Analysis (Welch's t-test and Mann-Whitney U)}
The comparative analysis across Tables 1 through 4 reveals distinct behavioral patterns based on project entry timing (Role) and long-term engagement (Tenure):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Founder vs. Late Joiner (Table 1):} Founders generally exhibit higher mean architectural exploration than Late Joiners. While early differences in \textit{Packages} and \textit{Methods} are not always significant via Welch's t-test at 5 commits, the Mann-Whitney U test shows significant stochastic dominance across all components and windows ($p < 0.001$), suggesting a fundamental difference in how early and late contributors engage with the system \cite{cite: 29}.
    \item \textbf{Moderate vs. Sustained (Table 2):} There is a highly significant difference ($p < 0.001$) between Moderate and Sustained developers across every metric \cite{cite: 33}. Sustained developers touch significantly more packages, classes, and methods even within their first 5 to 20 commits, identifying early-stage breadth as a primary differentiator for long-term retention \cite{cite: 33}.
    \item \textbf{Role impact within Tenure Groups (Tables 3-4):} Both Moderate and Sustained Founders outpace their Late Joiner counterparts \cite{cite: 36, 39}. The difference is particularly pronounced in \textit{Classes} and \textit{Methods} at the 20-commit mark ($p < 0.01$), indicating that Founders, regardless of their eventual stay, typically operate with a broader architectural scope than those joining later \cite{cite: 36, 39}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Variance and Interaction (Two-Way ANOVA)}
The ANOVA results (Tables 5 and 6) clarify the drivers of this architectural variation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Effect of Transients (Table 5 vs. 6):} Including Transient developers (those with $\le 10$ commits) significantly inflates the significance of \textit{Tenure} as a main effect \cite{cite: 66}. When Transients are excluded, the \textit{Tenure} effect often becomes non-significant for \textit{Packages} ($p = 0.825$ at 5 commits), suggesting that the primary behavioral gap exists between one-off contributors and everyone else, rather than between Moderate and Sustained regulars \cite{cite: 90}.
    \item \textbf{Component Sensitivity:} \textit{Methods} and \textit{Classes} consistently show significant main effects for \textit{Role} ($p < 0.05$) even after excluding transients \cite{cite: 90}. This confirms that the timing of project entry has a robust, lasting impact on how developers explore the code at a granular level \cite{cite: 90}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Predictive Power (Logistic Regression)}
The Logistic Regression model (Table 7) identifies architectural engagement as a viable predictor for long-term project commitment:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Effect Sizes:} All architectural components (Packages, Classes, and Methods) have Odds Ratios $> 1.0$, confirming that every additional unique component touched increases the likelihood of a developer becoming "Sustained" \cite{cite: 124, 126}.
    \item \textbf{Identification Thresholds:} Using a 30\% probability threshold, \textit{Classes} and \textit{Methods} at 20 commits prove to be high-recall predictors, identifying approximately 63.7\% and 58.6\% of future Sustained developers, respectively \cite{cite: 126}.
    \item \textbf{Model Accuracy:} The low "Moderate Met Threshold" rates ($\approx 2.3\%$) suggest the model is highly specific, rarely misidentifying Moderate developers as Sustained based on early-stage exploration \cite{cite: 126}.
\end{itemize}
